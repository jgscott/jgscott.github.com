Anomaly Is Not Causation

Why an unusual statistical pattern may justify scrutiny in litigation, but does not by itself identify the cause of the anomaly.
Author

James G. Scott

Published

February 12, 2025

Misconduct, or innocent anomaly?

Suppose a public agency investigates a series of bids submitted for emergency-facilities maintenance contracts. Two vendors have submitted bids with unusually high overlap across dozens of line items: the same hourly labor rates, the same equipment charges, the same materials prices, and the same markup on several highly specialized services. The agency finds this suspicious. It hires an expert to build a statistical model to evaluate whether the overlap is consistent with ordinary bidding or suggestive of coordination. The expert concludes that the observed overlap is extraordinarily unlikely if the vendors were acting independently.

This is one example of a recurring litigation pattern: a party observes conduct that looks unusual, retains an expert to model ordinary behavior, and then argues that the departure from ordinary behavior supports a legal claim. The same structure appears in fraud cases, where transaction patterns are said to depart from normal business activity; in antitrust cases, where pricing or bidding patterns are said to reflect coordination; and in employment cases, where pay, promotion, hiring, or discipline outcomes are compared to a benchmark. Across these settings, the statistical model is asked to convert “unusualness” or “improbability” into legal meaning.

The difficulty here is that “unlikely” and “caused by misconduct” are not the same conclusion. A model may show that the observed pattern is surprising under a particular account of ordinary behavior, while still leaving open the harder legal question: why did this pattern occur?

The calculation is only the first hard part

The probability calculation may look like the hard part. Often it is. Experts are retained because it takes real technical skill to define a baseline, build a model, and calculate how unusual the observed facts would be under that model.

It is tempting to think of a statistical model as a calculator: plug in the facts, press a button, and get back an objectively correct probability. But that is a misleading picture. A better way to think of a model is as a disciplined, but ultimately subjective, translation of a real-world story into mathematical form.

The model is the thing that gives precise meaning to the word “unlikely.” It specifies what ordinary bidding is assumed to look like, what sources of similarity are treated as expected, what sources of variation are treated as random, and what kinds of overlap are treated as surprising. Only after those assumptions are made does the probability calculation begin.

To continue with our bid-rigging example: a bid overlap cannot be deemed unlikely in the abstract. It can only be deemed unlikely relative to some mathematically precise account of how bids would have been prepared if the vendors were acting lawfully. That account may be simple or complex, good or bad, realistic or badly misspecified. But some account, some model of the world, is always there. Without it, there is no probability to calculate.

In cases like these, the model almost never asks, in any direct legal sense, whether the parties coordinated. It asks a narrower mathematical question: if these bidders were not coordinating, and if ordinary bidding worked in the particular way the model assumes, how likely would we be to see bid overlaps this extreme? The answer may be strikingly small. One in a thousand. One in a million. So rare that the expert compares it to selecting one particular inch on a journey to the sun and back twelve times.

Those numbers are meant to be daunting. They may even be mathematically correct, but only in the terms of the model. They tell us that the observed pattern is surprising under the model’s version of the world. They do not, by themselves, tell us why the surprising pattern occurred.

That is the central legal and inferential problem. Once an anomaly has been observed, more than one initially implausible explanation may become plausible. In our example of two companies bidding on hospital maintenance contracts, coordination is one possibility. But so are common suppliers, shared estimating software, public bid archives, standard labor assumptions, common subcontractor quotes, or a specialized market in which vendors face the same underlying costs. If, for example, there is only one company in the state qualified to service the cold head in a superconducting MRI magnet, two vendors may submit strikingly similar line items because both are passing through the same supplier quote.

Before seeing the bid overlap, any of those stories might have seemed improbable. But after seeing the overlap, several may deserve attention. The anomaly has resurrected more than one hypothesis. Said another way, a statistical model can show that something unusual happened. It cannot, by the mere fact of rarity, choose which unusual thing happened.

The missing steps

In this kind of case, the expert’s argument usually has several links. The practical danger is that a model used to prove surprise gets mistaken for a model that proves misconduct.

First, the model must give an operational account of ordinary, lawful conduct. The probability has to come from somewhere. This means the expert has to encode a specific mathematical version of what innocent bidding would look like. Would vendors price each line item independently? Would they draw from the same supplier lists? Would they use similar estimating software? Would they see the same public bid histories? Would they pass through subcontractor quotes in the same way? Would some categories of work have standardized rates while others vary widely? Until those choices are made, there is no probability to calculate. There is only a legal label, “no collusion,” waiting to be translated into a statistical model.

Those modeling choices can matter enormously. Under one version of innocent bidding, identical line items across vendors may look all but impossible. Under another, they may look merely uncommon. Under another, they may look routine for a subset of highly specialized services. A probability that appears devastating under one model, say one in a trillion, may become more like one in a hundred once the model allows for common suppliers, shared estimating software, or pass-through subcontractor quotes. Change the operational account of ordinary conduct, and the probability can move from “practically impossible” to “a bit unusual, but hardly shocking.” That does not mean the expert is free to choose whichever assumptions favor the client. It means the assumptions have to be exposed, defended, and tested against the data. The legal label “innocent bidding” is not enough. The model must say what innocent bidding is assumed to look like in the market the parties actually faced.

Second, the observed conduct must actually be anomalous under that model. This requires more than producing a small probability. The expert must identify the feature being tested: identical line items, overlapping markups, repeated pricing patterns, unusual timing, or some combination of these. The expert must also explain why that feature was chosen before the probability was calculated, rather than selected after the data were inspected.

This is a litigation version of an old statistical warning: if you torture the data long enough, it will confess to anything. A model can make almost anything look unusual if the target is defined after looking at the data. A bid pattern may look extraordinary if the expert searches across enough line items, enough vendors, enough contracts, enough time periods, and enough definitions of “overlap,” then reports only the most dramatic result.

For the same reason, the comparison set matters. A bid pattern may look extraordinary when compared to all vendors across all contracts. It may look much less extraordinary when compared to vendors bidding on the same specialized work, in the same region, under the same procurement rules, with access to the same public price information. A poorly chosen comparison set can make ordinary conduct look rare.

Third, the anomaly must be linked to the alleged misconduct. This is the attribution step. If the issue is collusion, the analysis cannot stop at showing that the bid pattern is unusual. It must explain why coordination is a better explanation than innocent mechanisms that could generate the same statistical pattern.

That usually requires more than the probability calculation itself. Common suppliers, shared subcontractor quotes, estimating software, public bid archives, standard cost books, and market conventions may all produce overlap. The model may account for those possibilities, rule them out, or ignore them. Those are very different things.

A statistical anomaly becomes more probative when it is tied to a credible mechanism: why the overlap appears on discretionary items rather than pass-through costs, why the same pattern recurs across bids, who had access to what information, and whether documents, communications, or business practices point in the same direction. Without that connection, the model may identify a pattern worth investigating. It has not yet identified misconduct.

A simple analogy

Suppose two top chefs in a sprawling city suddenly display an unexpected overlap in their supposedly unique seasonal menus: the same unusual squash, the same foraged mushroom, the same small-batch vinegar, the same obscure herb. The foodie accusations start to fly. A famous restaurant critic quickly labels the chefs unoriginal, a grave offense in the world of haute cuisine.

The chefs vigorously defend their honor. This, in turn, bruises the journalistic pride of the food critic so badly that he hires a statistician to substantiate his accusation. The statistician builds a complex model of “innocent” menu overlap, using menus from restaurants across the city, and finds that the overlap between these two chefs is extraordinarily rare. So rare, perhaps, that it is like choosing a specific grain of rice at random from all the sushi rolls ever eaten in Japan.

That finding may be correct. It may even be useful. But it is fundamentally a finding about detection, not attribution. The chefs might have copied each other. They might also have shopped at the same niche farmers’ market, bought from the same specialty distributor, followed the same supplier newsletter, or responded independently to a sudden surplus from a regional grower. Before seeing the menus, each of those explanations might have seemed unlikely. After seeing the overlap, several of them deserve attention. The statistician’s model has resurrected several possible explanations from the realm of the initially improbable. It has not selected the correct explanation.

The litigation takeaway

When an expert relies on statistical evidence of an anomaly, lawyers should press on the conversion from statistical rarity to legal meaning.

What is the model’s operational version of ordinary, lawful conduct? What feature of the data is being tested, and was that feature chosen before the analysis? What comparison set was used? Has the model been tested out of sample? What is its false-positive rate? What innocent mechanisms could produce the same pattern? And what evidence, apart from the small probability itself, links the anomaly to the alleged misconduct?

A strong anomaly analysis can identify something worth investigating. But to support a legal conclusion, it must do more. It must explain why the anomaly points to the alleged misconduct rather than to another mechanism that the anomaly itself has made newly plausible.
