Consulting and Expert Work

I consult on statistical, data-scientific, and machine-learning questions in litigation, regulatory, business, and policy settings. My role is to help attorneys and decision-makers understand what can and cannot be concluded from data, models, experiments, observational studies, algorithms, and statistical analyses.

Areas of focus

I am especially interested in matters involving:

  • Statistical modeling, uncertainty quantification, and interpretation of empirical evidence
  • Bayesian statistics, hierarchical models, shrinkage methods, and high-dimensional inference
  • Machine learning, algorithmic decision-making, generative AI, and model validation
  • Causal inference, observational studies, confounding, selection bias, and study design
  • Sampling, surveys, missing data, measurement error, and data quality
  • Multiple testing, false discoveries, anomaly detection, and pattern-finding in large data sets
  • Public-health, medical, policy, labor, business, and technology-related data analyses

Litigation support

I can assist counsel at different stages of a matter, including early case assessment, discovery planning, expert reports, deposition preparation, rebuttal analysis, and testimony. Typical work may include:

  • Evaluating the statistical validity of claims made by opposing experts
  • Reviewing analyses, code, data pipelines, and methodological assumptions
  • Designing or critiquing empirical studies
  • Translating technical statistical issues into clear language for attorneys, courts, regulators, or business audiences
  • Preparing affirmative expert opinions where the statistical evidence supports them

Representative questions

Examples of questions where statistical expertise may be useful include:

  • Does the analysis actually support the causal claim being made?
  • Were the data sampled, filtered, linked, or transformed in a way that could bias the result?
  • Are the model assumptions appropriate for the decision being made?
  • Does a machine-learning system perform reliably on the relevant population?
  • Are reported error rates, confidence intervals, posterior intervals, or p-values being interpreted correctly?
  • Has the analyst accounted for multiple comparisons, selection effects, missingness, or measurement error?
  • Could an apparent pattern be explained by chance, data processing choices, or model instability?
